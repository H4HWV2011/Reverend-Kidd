Strangers on the ridge: surveyors and land agents

A few years after the war, different kinds of men start showing up:

    Railroad surveyors:

        Men with tripods, chains, notebooks, and strange instruments, walking ridges and creek bottoms, driving stakes into the earth.

        They talk about “grades,” “rights‑of‑way,” and “connecting to main lines,” sketching lines that will become future tracks.

    Company men / land agents:

        Well‑dressed compared to locals, sometimes with law papers and ledgers, sometimes just with cash and contracts.

        They ask: “Who owns this ridge? Who holds title to that hollow?” then start offering money for mineral rights—coal they say the farmers will never use themselves.

From his perspective:

    He sees neighbors who rarely saw cash suddenly holding dollars in hand, tempted to sell something they can’t see—coal under the ground—in exchange for immediate relief.

    He hears words he’s never heard in sermons or farm talk: corporation, lease, royalty, deed language designed to confuse.

Young men pulled from fields to timber and coal

At first, it’s timber:

    Companies want ties, trestles, and mine props; loggers offer wages to cut trees that have stood for generations.

    Young men, tired of hard scrabble farming with no cash, go to work felling trees and floating or hauling logs, bringing home money but also injuries and new dependencies.

Then coal follows the rails:

    Small drift mines and slopes open near future towns like those around Pax and Paint Creek.

    Recruiters promise steady wages, perhaps housing, store credit—more immediate security than a worn‑out hillside can offer.

    Boys he baptized and counseled leave fields fallow to work underground.

He watches a pattern he recognizes from the war:

    External forces pulling local men out of the hollow into dangerous systems.

    Families increasingly dependent on outsiders for survival—before it was armies and war contracts; now it’s time‑clock wages and company script.

A new master: corporate power instead of plantation or army

He has lived under:

    The old planter aristocracy (rich lowland landholders controlling politics and law).

    The armies and wartime governments that claimed bodies and loyalty.

    Now he sees a third regime: corporate capital, coal companies, and railroads.

Differences and eerie similarities:

    No one is holding a gun on the courthouse steps now; instead, men hold contracts and ledgers that can evict a family as effectively as a rifle.

    Instead of a uniform and flag, the new masters have logos, company stores, and bosses.

    Instead of conscription, there is economic compulsion: if you don’t go to work for them, how will you buy salt, tools, cloth?

From the pulpit and porch conversations, he:

    Warns, gently, that selling mineral rights or taking on company debt is not just a business matter but a spiritual and communal one—who you let own your future.

    Draws analogies between Pharaoh’s grain policies or biblical kings who traded away people’s land, and the contracts now circulating in his hills.

    Sees that some folks think he’s overreacting—“Preacher, this is progress”—while others feel a dim unease they can’t articulate.

His late‑life vantage point

By now, he’s an old man:

    He has buried Confederate and Union dead, now he starts burying loggers and miners killed by falling trees and early mine accidents.

    He notes that the widows’ tears look the same, whether the death came on a battlefield or under a slab of coal.

He realizes:

    In his youth, the main threat was distance from power—no roads, no help.

    Now, the threat is over‑proximity to power—railroads and companies burrowing into every hollow.

    The constant is that mountain people must learn how to deal with outsiders who want what’s under their feet or in their bodies—labor, land, or coal.

His preaching shifts:

    Less about the big national dramas (which he’s seen chew up his people twice now), more about wisdom, caution, and mutual support:

        “Do not sign what you do not understand.”

        “Do not let hunger today cost your grandchildren tomorrow.”

        “Remember: our worth is not in their ledgers.”

This chapter shows:

    He lives long enough to see the mountains move from war zones to resource zones, and he recognizes that although the uniforms changed to suits, the hunger of the outside world stayed the same. He spends his last working years trying to prepare his flock for a quieter, more seductive kind of conquest—the one that comes with wages and contracts instead of drums and guns.
