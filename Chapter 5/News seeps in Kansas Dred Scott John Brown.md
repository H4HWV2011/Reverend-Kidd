News seeps in: Kansas, Dred Scott, John Brown

Information arrives slowly but with force:

    Traveling merchants, peddlers, and drovers bring stories from towns and newspapers—“Bleeding Kansas,” where men are killing over whether a territory will be slave or free.

    A tattered paper or someone who can read aloud shares the Dred Scott decision: the highest court says Black people “have no rights which the white man is bound to respect.” That phrase lodges in people’s minds like a splinter.

    Rumors of John Brown’s raid at Harpers Ferry (1859) arrive: an abolitionist trying to spark a slave uprising not so far away in the mountains of Virginia.

Around campfires, millstones, and porch rails, hollow men argue:

    Some say, “Slavery’s wrong before God; always has been.”

    Others: “It’s the law, it’s the way of things down east; what business is it of the government or Yankees?”

    Most don’t own slaves but understand that whatever happens to the powerful Tidewater class will eventually hit them through taxes, war, or law.

Arguments in the congregation

These national fractures walk straight into his meeting house:

    Prayers start to include pointed language: “Deliver us from tyranny” or “Protect our property and rights,” depending on who is praying.

    Men who once only fought over fences now fight about the Union, whether Washington has overstepped, whether talk of secession is righteous defiance or madness.

    Some younger men are drawn to fiery rhetoric about honor and defending home; others mutter that the poor always bleed for the rich man’s quarrels.

He sees:

    Friendships strain as one man reads an anti‑slavery tract and another repeats pro‑slavery sermons from a visiting preacher.

    Women worry aloud: “If there’s war, it’ll be our boys that go—what do we even believe about this?”

The meeting house, once a refuge from outside noise, becomes a place where outside noise is argued through with God as referee.
His struggle in the pulpit

He stands in front of them with three pressures:

    Scripture: he knows the Bible’s language about justice, the oppressed, loving enemies, obeying authorities, and also the stories of Israel resisting bad kings.

    Community: he loves people on both sides of these arguments—they are his kin, his flock, not abstractions.

    Real danger: he knows that taking too clear a side could invite retaliation—from neighbors, from authorities, or both.

So he aims his sermons at heart and conscience, not party:

    Preaches on repentance for personal sin—drunkenness, cruelty, dishonesty—pointing out that a man’s stance on national issues means little if his home life is rotten.

    Preaches on unity in Christ, warning that if they devour each other now, no outside army will need to destroy them.

    Uses Old Testament stories—civil war in Israel, kings who led people astray—to frame the moment as spiritual testing, not simple political choice.

But the more he leans into “repent and love one another,” the more some hear avoidance:

    The hot‑blooded accuse him of being lukewarm, unwilling to call injustice by its name.

    The cautious worry he’s saying too much when he even hints that no earthly nation is the Kingdom of God.

He feels the ground shifting:

    The same call he felt as a boy now carries new risk—his words might push men toward forgiveness or toward picking up rifles.

    He starts to sense that a time is coming when “preaching unity” won’t be enough, and that scares him more than any personal danger.

This chapter shows:

    War arrives first as rumor and argument, not bullets. The Reverend is trying to hold a fragile community together with scripture and common sense while the wider country splits; he can feel that his hollow will not be able to stay neutral forever.
